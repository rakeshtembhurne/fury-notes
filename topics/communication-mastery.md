# Communication Mastery

## Source

## Key Takeaways

1. **Brain processes 400 words/min but we speak at 125 words/min** — minds wander 47% of the time during conversation
2. **Curiosity is triggered by the "information gap"** — knowing almost enough, not nothing
3. **Don't frontload context — lead with the payload** — inverted pyramid structure
4. **Concrete beats abstract** — specific images are more memorable than summaries
5. **To be interesting, be interested** — best listeners are the most magnetic
6. **The biggest barrier to being memorable isn't how you talk — it's how you listen**

## The Information Gap Theory

> "Curiosity isn't triggered by knowing nothing. It's triggered by knowing almost enough — a partial answer, a question, but not the resolution."

- The brain is wired to close gaps
- Lead with the gap, not the answer
- Create a reason to care before explaining

---

## Communication Mistakes

### Burying the Lead
"So, okay, a little background. I've been working on this for a few months, and there's a lot of history here, but basically what happened was back in 2019..."

**The fix:** Be short. Be sweet. Start with the spice.

### Abstract Over Concrete
"Your meeting skills need improvement" vs. "Last week your presentation put two people to sleep and one walked out"

**The fix:** Use specific images, vivid details, real moments. The brain stores concrete, forgets abstract.

### Chronological Storytelling
Your brain wants to tell stories chronologically. That feels natural to you but is torturous to your listener.

**The fix:** Inverted pyramid — lead with the most important info first.

## The Art of Being Interesting

### The Information Processing Gap
- Brain processes 400 words/min
- Average speech rate: 125 words/min
- Gap = wandering mind
- You must give the brain something to hold onto

### How to Hold Attention
1. **Lead with the hook** — open with the key point or question
2. **Use pauses** — silence forces attention back
3. **Be concrete** — specific images > abstract summaries
4. **Create information gaps** — partial answers, unresolved questions

> "If you want to put someone to sleep — don't vary your tone. If you want them awake — use the pause."

---

## The Self-Interest Principle

> "Information is remembered dramatically better when it's processed in relation to the self."

Before you explain something, create a reason to care. Ask: "How does this apply to me?" — then answer that question for them.

---

## The Listening Trap

> "The biggest barrier to being memorable isn't how you talk — it's how you listen."

- Almost everyone described as "magnetic" or "captivating" is universally the **best listener in the room** — not the best talker
- To be remembered as a great conversationalist, you actually just need to talk less
- **Best listeners:** observe, ask questions, stay in the question longer

## Action Items

- [ ] Before speaking, ask "What's my one key point?" — lead with that
- [ ] Add more pauses — let silence work for you
- [ ] Replace abstract statements with concrete images
- [ ] Practice "staying in the question" — listen to respond, not to reply
- [ ] Next conversation: ask a question before giving an answer
- [ ] Identify your "default" communication style — are you burying the lead?
